Study Guide Notes

Nuremberg Trials (1945–1949)

  • Held to prosecute Nazi war criminals for crimes against humanity, war crimes, and genocide after WWII.
  • To ensure accountability for the atrocities committed during the Holocaust and WWII.
  • Established legal precedents for international law and human rights.
  • Led to the creation of the International Criminal Court and the principle of individual responsibility for war crimes.

Effects of the GI Bill (1944) on the U.S. Economy

  • Increased education and training for veterans, leading to a more skilled workforce.
  • Boosted homeownership through low-interest loans, stimulating the housing market.
  • Contributed to economic growth in the post-WWII era by expanding the middle class and driving consumer demand.

Post WWII population in US

  • Baby Boom (1946–1964): A significant rise in birth rates, adding over 76 million people to the U.S. population.
  • Led to a youthful, growing workforce, fueling economic growth and increasing demand for goods and services.
  • Contributed to the rise of suburbanization, as families moved to newly developed areas.

President who won against Truman | Dwight D. Eisenhower(1952)

  • Defeated Adlai Stevenson, ending Democratic control of the White House after Truman.
  • Ran on a platform of anti-communism, government efficiency, and ending the Korean War.
  • His victory marked a shift toward Republican leadership during the Cold War.

creation of NASA (1958)

  • Sputnik Shock (1957): The Soviet Union's launch of Sputnik 1 sparked fears of U.S. technological inferiority.
  • Cold War Competition: The U.S. aimed to catch up in the space race and demonstrate scientific and military strength.
  • National Defense & Innovation: NASA was established to coordinate space exploration, leading to advancements in technology and aerospace.

Formation of SEATO (1954)

  • Created to counter communist expansion in Southeast Asia, especially after the Korean War and the fall of French Indochina.
  • Modeled after NATO, it aimed to provide collective defense against communist threats.
  • Included the U.S., UK, France, and several Asia-Pacific nations, but lacked strong military enforcement and eventually dissolved in 1977.

Peace Corps and how it related to the Cold War (1961-now)

  • Established by President Kennedy to send American volunteers abroad for education, health, and development work.
  • Aimed to promote U.S. goodwill and counter communist influence in developing nations.
  • Served as a soft power tool in the Cold War, showing democracy and capitalism as beneficial alternatives to communism.

Impact of Advertising Post-WWII

  • Boosted consumer culture, promoting mass production and consumption of goods.
  • Advertisements heavily targeted women, encouraging domestic products and family-centered lifestyles.
  • Also aimed at youth, emphasizing freedom and individuality through products like cars and fashion.
  • Led to the rise of brand loyalty and contributed to the growth of suburban life and consumerism.

Influence of Beatnik and Counterculture Movements (1950s–1960s)

  • Beatniks: Challenged traditional norms with their embrace of alternative lifestyles, jazz, poetry, and rejection of materialism.
  • Counterculture Movement: Rejected mainstream American values, especially in areas like civil rights, Vietnam War, and sexual liberation.
  • Sparked broader youth rebellion, leading to the 1960s counterculture, influencing music, art, and politics.

Joseph McCarthy and McCarthyism

Joseph McCarthy:

  • senator claiming to have a list of communists in the government
  • became the face of anticommunism hysteria in the early 50's
  • made a lot of aggressive and baseless accusations to get in power

McCarthyism:

  • making accusations of communism with no real evidence
  • led to government investigations, blacklists, and ruined careers
  • relied on fear and intimidation rather than proof

Impact:

  • people scared and worried about politics and media in their everyday life

Loyalty review boards

  • made by President Truman in 1947 (Executive Order 9835)

  • investigate federal employees for communist ties
  • employees could be dismissed if they were deemed "security risk"
  • mostly worked based on suspicion
  • a bunch of people got investigated, and fired without good proof of wrongdoing

Ethel and Julius Rosenberg (1951–1953)

  • Convicted of espionage for allegedly passing atomic secrets to the Soviet Union.
  • Executed in 1953, becoming the first U.S. civilians executed for espionage during peacetime.
  • Their trial and execution sparked debates over civil liberties and the intensity of the Red Scare.

U.S. involvement in the Korean War (1950-1953)

  • North Korea invaded South Korea (backed by USSR and China)
  • US intended to stop the spread of communism
  • Fought under the UN coalition, framing it as a defense of democracy
  • War began on June 25, 1950, with North Korea’s invasion
  • General Douglas MacArthur led a successful counterattack but was later removed for pushing to invade China.
  • Stalemate led to an armistice in 1953, with the border remaining near the 38th parallel.
  • Korea remained divided into North (communist) and South (capitalist).
  • Set a precedent for U.S. intervention in Cold War conflicts.
  • Increased military spending and solidified tensions between the U.S., USSR, and China.

Gulf of Tonkin Incident & Resolution (1964)

  • Alleged attacks on U.S. ships by North Vietnam led to the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution.
  • Gave President Lyndon B. Johnson broad authority to escalate U.S. involvement in Vietnam without a formal war declaration.
  • Led to full-scale U.S. military intervention, later criticized for expanding executive power and being based on questionable evidence.

Truman Doctrine and Marshall plan (compare)

    Truman Doctrine 1947

  • Contain communism by supporting nations that resist soviet influence
  • focused on military and economic aid to Greece and Turkey
  • Polcy marked the start of U.S. Cold War interventionism

    The Marshall Plan 1948

  • rebuild war-torn Europe to prevent communist influence
  • Focus on economic recovery, over $13 billion in aid
  • Strengthened Western Europe's economies, reducing soviet appeal

    Similarities

  • contain communism
  • involved U.S. financial aid to foreign nations
  • Strengthened U.S. influence in Europe

    • Differences

  • Truman Doc: focused on military and direct intervention
  • Marshall Plan: focused on economic aid and rebuilding
  • Truman: Targeted Greece and Turkey
  • Marshall Plan: applied to all western europe

Soviet Expansion and U.S. Foreign Policy (1947/1991)

  • U.S. adopted Containment Doctrine to prevent communism's spread.
  • Intervened in conflicts (e.g., Korean War, Vietnam War) and supported anti-communist regimes.
  • Formed NATO and provided economic/military aid (e.g., Marshall Plan) to counter Soviet influence.

Soviet Union (1949)

  • The Soviet Union became the second nation to acquire atomic weapons, successfully testing their first bomb on August 29, 1949.
  • This event intensified the Cold War and sparked the nuclear arms race between the U.S. and the USSR.

Hydrogen Bomb vs. Atomic Bomb

  • Atomic Bomb (A-Bomb):
    • Uses nuclear fission (splitting atoms) to release energy.
    • Less powerful than the hydrogen bomb.
  • Hydrogen Bomb (H-Bomb):
    • Uses nuclear fusion (combining atoms) to release much more energy.
    • Far more powerful than an atomic bomb and capable of much greater destruction.

    Causes and Effects of the Arms Race (1945–1991)

      • Causes:
      • Competition between the U.S. and the Soviet Union for global dominance during the Cold War.
      • Desire to maintain military superiority, especially with nuclear weapons.
      • Effects:
      • Escalation of nuclear weapons development, leading to stockpiles of devastating weapons.
      • Heightened global tensions, contributing to the risk of nuclear war and the establishment of military alliances like NATO and the Warsaw Pact.

    The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)

    • U.S. found soviet missles in Cuba, led to a 13-day standoff
    • Kennedy imposed a naval blockade demanding removal of the missles
    • Soviets agreed in exchange for not invading Cuba and removing missles from Turkey
    • showed the dangers of nuclear confrontation
    • led to establishing the Hotline Agreement (direct comms hotline)
    • accelerated arms control efforts, partial nuclear test ban treaty (1963)

    Containment Policy (1947–1991)

    • U.S. strategy to prevent the spread of communism during the Cold War.
    • First implemented through the Truman Doctrine and Marshall Plan, later influencing conflicts like Korea and Vietnam.
    • Led to U.S. military, economic, and political interventions worldwide to counter Soviet influence.

    Geneva Accords and the Vietnam War (1954)

    • Divided Vietnam at the 17th parallel, with North Vietnam (communist) and South Vietnam (anti-communist).
    • Called for elections in 1956 to reunify Vietnam, but the U.S. and South Vietnam refused, fearing a communist victory.
    • Led to increasing U.S. involvement, eventually escalating into the Vietnam War.

    U.S. Involvement in Vietnam (1955–1975)

    • Aimed to contain communism after the Geneva Accords divided Vietnam.
    • Supported South Vietnam against the communist Viet Cong and North Vietnam.
    • Escalated after the Gulf of Tonkin Incident (1964), leading to full-scale U.S. military involvement.

    Protests Against the War

    • Anti-war movement grew in the late 1960s, fueled by the draft, high casualties, and media coverage.
    • Major protests included the 1968 Democratic National Convention and Kent State shootings (1970).
    • Public opposition pressured the government to withdraw troops.

    Effects of the war

    • Vietnam unified under communism after U.S. withdrawal in 1973 and the fall of Saigon (1975).
    • Massive U.S. casualities, over 58,000 dead, and deep public distrust in the government
    • Led to War Powers Act (1973), limiting presidential military authority without congressional approval.

    Watergate and its impact | Watergate Scandal (1972–1974)

    • Break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters led to an investigation uncovering widespread corruption.
    • President Nixon resigned in 1974 to avoid impeachment after it was revealed he attempted to cover up the scandal.
    • Eroded public trust in the government
    • Led to stronger oversight measures like Freeom of Information Act and War Powers Act
    • Set a precedent for presidential accountability
